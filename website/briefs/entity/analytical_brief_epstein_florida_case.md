**2005-2006: Palm Beach Police Investigation**

**July 7, 2008: CVRA Case Filed**

**ECF Doc. 1330-1, filed 01/05/24:**

Attorney Bradley J. Edwards filed an emergency petition to enforce the rights of "Jane Doe No. 1" and "Jane Doe No. 2" under the Crime Victims' Rights Act (CVRA), 18 U.S.C. § 3771, alleging that the Government had failed to provide them rights with regard to a plea arrangement it was pursuing with Epstein.

The petition alleged:

> "the U.S. Attorney's Office for the Southern District of Florida had concealed from Epstein's victims a non-prosecution agreement (NPA) that they had reached with Epstein."

**December 30, 2014: Jane Doe #3 and #4 Seek to Join**

**ECF Doc. 1325-3, filed 01/04/24:**

Attorneys Bradley J. Edwards and Paul G. Cassell filed a motion for Jane Doe #3 and Jane Doe #4 to join the CVRA action. The motion included detailed allegations:

Regarding Jane Doe #4:

> "an economically poor and vulnerable sixteen-year-old child" who was told "she could make $300 cash by giving an old man a massage on Palm Beach" in or around summer 2002. The motion alleged Epstein himself got on the phone with Jane Doe #4, "asking her personally to come to his mansion in Palm Beach." During her first visit, "Epstein asked Jane Doe #[4] her age and she told him she had recently turned sixteen. ### The Non-Prosecution Agreement

**Key Features:**

According to court filings, the NPA:

1. **Was kept secret from victims** until after it was finalized
2. **Protected Epstein** from federal prosecution
3. **Protected unnamed "co-conspirators"** from federal prosecution
4. **Resulted in state charges only** — Epstein pleaded guilty to solicitation of prostitution
5. **Allowed work release** during his 13-month county jail sentence

### Court Rulings

**2013 CVRA Ruling:**

The Court concluded that CVRA rights extended to Jane Doe #1 and Jane Doe #2 even though federal charges were never filed. Because the NPA barred prosecution of crimes committed against them by Epstein, they had "standing" to assert violations of their CVRA rights.

**April 7, 2015:**

The Court denied Jane Doe #3 and Jane Doe #4's motion to join the litigation.

## Editorial Analysis

**The following represents the opinions and interpretations of The Continuum Report.**

**Regarding the NPA:** In our assessment, the 2008 non-prosecution agreement represents a significant failure of the justice system to protect victims and hold a wealthy, well-connected defendant accountable. The agreement's secrecy from victims, its protection of unnamed co-conspirators, and its lenient terms have been widely criticized.

**Regarding the CVRA litigation:** The victims' decision to pursue CVRA litigation established important precedent regarding victims' rights during criminal investigations. The case demonstrated that victims have enforceable rights even before charges are filed.

**Regarding the evidence spoliation:** The testimony regarding computer removal before police arrival, and Maxwell's call to the housekeeper on the morning of the warrant execution, raises questions about potential obstruction. We note these facts appear in court filings but draw no conclusions about criminal liability.

**Regarding prosecutorial decision-making:** The NPA was negotiated by the U.S. Attorney's Office under then-U.S. Attorney Alexander Acosta, who later served as Secretary of Labor until resigning in 2019 amid renewed scrutiny of the Epstein matter. We observe that the decision-making process leading to the NPA has never been fully explained publicly.

**Our editorial judgment:** The documentary record supports characterizing the 2008 NPA as an unusually favorable outcome for a defendant facing serious allegations of sex trafficking of minors. The victims' CVRA litigation brought important scrutiny to this prosecutorial decision and contributed to the eventual reopening of federal investigation.
