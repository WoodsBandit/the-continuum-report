## Alternative Interpretations

Reasonable observers might interpret this evidence differently:

1. **Documentation protocols as dispositive:** The Secret Service's mandatory documentation requirements may represent highly reliable evidence. If agents are required to file logs and vouchers for all travel with a protectee, the absence of such documentation may conclusively establish that certain travel did not occur.

2. **Documentation may have limitations:** Secret Service documentation requirements may not apply uniformly to all circumstances. A former president might travel in some contexts without full Secret Service accompaniment or documentation, particularly to private international locations.

3. **Adversarial investigation context:** The Louis Freeh report was commissioned by Maxwell's defense team. While Freeh's former position as FBI Director lends credibility, the adversarial context in which the investigation was conducted is relevant to evaluating its conclusions.

4. **Classification considerations:** Some Secret Service records may be classified or otherwise exempt from disclosure. The "total absence" of documentation in available records does not necessarily mean no documentation exists in classified holdings.

5. **Time period specificity:** The Freeh report addressed a specific time period (January 1, 2001 to January 1, 2003). Travel outside this date range would not be addressed by this finding.

6. **Source limitation:** Our analysis is based on Maxwell's characterization of the Freeh report, not the report itself. The actual Freeh report was not included in the publicly available court record we reviewed.

7. **Institutional records vs. individual memory:** The Secret Service reference illustrates a broader evidentiary tension between contemporaneous institutional records and individual witness recollections. Institutional records may be more reliable than memories of events years prior.
