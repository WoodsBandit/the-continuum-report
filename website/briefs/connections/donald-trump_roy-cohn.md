## The Documented Record

**The following contains only factual citations. No interpretation.**

- Roy Cohn and Donald Trump met in 1973 at Le Club, an exclusive Manhattan nightclub.
- Cohn became Trump's personal attorney and remained so for approximately 13 years until Cohn's death in 1986.
- Cohn represented Trump and Trump Management Corporation in a 1973 Department of Justice lawsuit alleging racial discrimination in housing practices. Cohn filed a $100 million countersuit against the government.
- The discrimination case was settled in 1975 with a consent decree requiring changes to rental practices, but no admission of wrongdoing.
- Cohn introduced Trump to political figures, media personalities, and business contacts in New York City.
- Cohn represented Trump in various business dealings throughout the 1970s and early 1980s, including real estate negotiations and casino licensing matters.
- Trump visited Cohn during his final illness but reportedly distanced himself as Cohn's health deteriorated and his disbarment proceedings advanced.
- Cohn was disbarred in New York in June 1986 for unethical conduct and died of AIDS-related complications in August 1986.
- Trump has publicly credited Cohn with teaching him to fight back aggressively against opponents and never apologize.

## Editorial Analysis

**The following represents the opinions and interpretations of The Continuum Report.**

The Cohn-Trump relationship represents one of the most consequential mentor-protege dynamics in modern American political history. Cohn, already a controversial figure from his role as chief counsel to Senator Joseph McCarthy during the 1950s Red Scare hearings, found in the young real estate developer an eager student of his combative philosophy.

Cohn's influence on Trump appears to have been profound and lasting. The tactical playbook Trump would later employ in business and politics—aggressive counterattacks, litigation as a weapon, manipulation of media narratives, and categorical denial regardless of evidence—mirrors Cohn's own methodology.

The relationship also connected Trump to Cohn's extensive network spanning organized crime figures, political power brokers, and media personalities. This network provided Trump with access and connections that accelerated his rise in New York real estate and tabloid culture.

The manner in which the relationship ended—with Trump reportedly distancing himself from his dying mentor—has been cited by some observers as indicative of Trump's transactional approach to personal relationships.
