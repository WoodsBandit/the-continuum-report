## The Documented Record

**The following contains only direct quotes and factual citations. No interpretation.**

### Source: United States v. Keith Raniere, Case No. 18-CR-204 (E.D.N.Y.)

Keith Raniere was the founder and leader of NXIVM, a purported self-improvement organization based in Albany, New York. He founded the organization in 1998 with Nancy Salzman.

Raniere was arrested on March 26, 2018, in Puerto Vallarta, Mexico, by Mexican federal police acting in coordination with the FBI. He was subsequently extradited to the United States.

The federal indictment charged Raniere with:
- Racketeering
- Racketeering conspiracy
- Wire fraud conspiracy
- Forced labor conspiracy
- Sex trafficking
- Sex trafficking conspiracy
- Attempted sex trafficking

Prosecutors established that Raniere created and led DOS (Dominus Obsequious Sororium), a secret society within NXIVM operating as a master-slave hierarchy with Raniere as the sole "grandmaster." DOS members were required to provide "collateral" including compromising photographs and were branded with a symbol containing Raniere's initials.

The trial commenced on May 7, 2019, before Judge Nicholas G. Garaufis in the United States District Court for the Eastern District of New York. Testimony was presented over approximately six weeks.

On June 19, 2019, the jury found Keith Raniere guilty on all counts after less than five hours of deliberation.

On October 27, 2020, Judge Garaufis sentenced Raniere to 120 years in federal prison. The court also imposed a $1.75 million fine and ordered restitution to victims.

Raniere was also ordered to pay restitution totaling $3.5 million to 21 victims.

Raniere is incarcerated at United States Penitentiary Tucson in Arizona. He has appealed his conviction, with appeals pending as of 2024.

## Editorial Analysis

**The following represents the opinions and interpretations of The Continuum Report.**

Keith Raniere's prosecution represents one of the most significant federal cases involving a purported self-improvement organization operating as a criminal enterprise. The 120-year sentence effectively ensures Raniere will spend the remainder of his life in federal prison.

The trial revealed a pattern of manipulation and control spanning decades, with Raniere allegedly exploiting individuals seeking personal development and professional advancement. The DOS structure, with its branding, collateral, and hierarchy, demonstrated the extreme nature of control Raniere exercised over members.

The conviction on all counts, achieved after a relatively brief jury deliberation, suggests the evidence presented was overwhelming. Multiple cooperating witnesses, including former members of Raniere's inner circle, provided testimony detailing his methods and conduct.

The case has broader implications for understanding and identifying organizations that present as self-improvement groups while engaging in exploitation. The extensive media coverage and documentary attention has raised public awareness about warning signs of high-demand organizations.
