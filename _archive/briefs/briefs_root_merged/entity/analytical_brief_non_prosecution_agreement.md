## Statement of Public Interest

The 2008 Non-Prosecution Agreement between the U.S. Attorney's Office for the Southern District of Florida and Jeffrey Epstein represents one of the most controversial prosecutorial decisions in modern American legal history. The agreement allowed a defendant facing potential federal sex trafficking charges involving multiple minor victims to plead guilty to state prostitution charges, serve approximately 13 months in county jail with work release privileges, and avoid federal prosecution entirely. The NPA also granted immunity to unnamed "potential co-conspirators" whose identities were never publicly disclosed.

In February 2019, U.S. District Judge Kenneth Marra ruled that federal prosecutors violated the Crime Victims' Rights Act (CVRA) by negotiating the agreement in secret without notifying identified victims. This ruling was subsequently reversed by the Eleventh Circuit Court of Appeals in 2021. The DOJ Office of Professional Responsibility investigated the conduct of the prosecutors involved and concluded they exercised "poor judgment" but did not commit professional misconduct.

The NPA is a matter of extraordinary public concern because it directly enabled Epstein to avoid federal accountability for over a decade, until his 2019 arrest in New York. The document has been released through court proceedings and is part of the public record. This analysis is offered in furtherance of informed public discourse regarding prosecutorial accountability and victims' rights.

## Executive Summary

**Editorial Assessment:** In our assessment, the 2008 Non-Prosecution Agreement represents a watershed moment in the Epstein case—the point at which a federal investigation into sex trafficking of minors was diverted into a state plea deal that many observers have characterized as extraordinarily lenient. Based on our review of the documentary record, the NPA's key features—secret negotiation, victim exclusion, co-conspirator immunity, and minimal incarceration—appear, in our view, to have prioritized closure over accountability.

The agreement was negotiated between U.S. Attorney Alexander Acosta's office and Epstein's defense team, which included prominent attorneys Jay Lefkowitz, Kenneth Starr, and Alan Dershowitz. Victims were not notified until after the agreement was finalized, in what Judge Marra later characterized as a deliberate decision to "conceal the existence of the [NPA] and mislead the victims."

We observe that the NPA's co-conspirator immunity provision has never been fully explained. The identities of individuals protected by this immunity were not publicly disclosed. When Ghislaine Maxwell was arrested in 2020, prosecutors in the Southern District of New York—not Florida—brought the charges, potentially avoiding NPA complications.

The declaration further states:

> "the U.S. Attorney's Office for the Southern District of Florida had concealed from Epstein's victims a non-prosecution agreement (NPA) that they had reached with Epstein."

### Judge Kenneth Marra's Ruling (February 21, 2019)

U.S. District Judge Kenneth Marra ruled that federal prosecutors violated the Crime Victims' Rights Act when they secretly negotiated the NPA without notifying identified victims.

From Judge Marra's ruling (as cited in public records):

> "When the Government gives information to victims, it cannot be misleading. While the Government spent untold hours negotiating the terms and implications of the NPA with Epstein's attorneys, scant information was shared with victims. Instead, the victims were told to be 'patient' while the investigation proceeded."

Judge Marra found:

> "made a decision to conceal the existence of the [non-prosecution agreement] and mislead the victims to believe that federal prosecution was still a possibility."

### Eleventh Circuit Reversal (2021)

**[Wild v. DOJ — 11th Circuit Opinion](https://thecontinuumreport.com/sources/florida-case/2020-WILD-V-DOJ-11TH-CIRCUIT-EPSTEIN-CVRA.pdf)**, dated April 14, 2020:

The Eleventh Circuit Court of Appeals, sitting en banc, reversed Judge Marra's CVRA ruling. The appellate court held that CVRA rights do not attach until formal charges are filed. Because federal charges were never filed against Epstein, the appeals court found that no CVRA violation occurred as a matter of law.

This reversal did not address the conduct of prosecutors; it held that the statute did not provide the victims with enforceable rights at the pre-charging stage.

### DOJ Office of Professional Responsibility Report (November 2020)

**[DOJ OPR Report](https://thecontinuumreport.com/sources/florida-case/2020-DOJ-OPR-REPORT-EPSTEIN-NPA-INVESTIGATION.pdf)**:

The Department of Justice Office of Professional Responsibility conducted an investigation into the conduct of Alexander Acosta and other prosecutors involved in the 2008 NPA. The report concluded:

- Prosecutors exercised "poor judgment" in several aspects of the plea agreement
- The prosecutors did not commit professional misconduct
- The prosecutors did not violate any laws
- The investigation did not find grounds for disciplinary action

### Outcome of the NPA

As a result of the agreement:

## Editorial Analysis

**The following represents the opinions and interpretations of The Continuum Report.**

**Regarding the NPA's terms:** In our view, the 2008 Non-Prosecution Agreement represents an extraordinary departure from typical federal prosecution of sex crimes involving multiple minor victims. The combination of federal immunity, co-conspirator protection, state-level charges, minimal jail time, and work release privileges appears, in our assessment, to have been uniquely favorable to a defendant with substantial financial and legal resources.

**Regarding victim exclusion:** The decision to negotiate the NPA without notifying identified victims—while spending "untold hours" with defense counsel—suggests, in our interpretation, that victims' statutory rights were subordinated to the interests of reaching a quiet resolution. Judge Marra's finding that prosecutors deliberately concealed the NPA and misled victims represents a significant judicial indictment of the process, even though this finding was later reversed on jurisdictional grounds.

**Regarding co-conspirator immunity:** We observe that the NPA's blanket immunity for "potential co-conspirators" remains one of its most troubling features. The identities of individuals protected by this provision were never publicly disclosed. This immunity may have shielded individuals who facilitated Epstein's crimes from federal prosecution for over a decade. When Ghislaine Maxwell was arrested in 2020, prosecutors in the Southern District of New York brought charges, potentially avoiding NPA complications.

**Regarding prosecutorial decision-making:** The NPA was negotiated under U.S. Attorney Alexander Acosta, who later served as Secretary of Labor until resigning in July 2019 amid renewed scrutiny. Acosta defended the agreement at a 2019 press conference, arguing that the alternative might have been no jail time at all. In our view, this explanation does not adequately address why co-conspirators received immunity, why victims were not consulted, or why the agreement was kept secret.

**Regarding the 11th Circuit reversal:** The appellate reversal of Judge Marra's ruling was based on statutory interpretation—that CVRA rights do not attach until charges are filed—not on the merits of prosecutorial conduct. The reversal does not vindicate the prosecutors' behavior; it holds that victims had no enforceable legal remedy. In our assessment, this represents a significant gap in victims' rights law.

**Regarding the OPR finding:** The Office of Professional Responsibility concluded that prosecutors exercised "poor judgment" but committed no misconduct. We observe that "poor judgment" is an unusual institutional acknowledgment from DOJ regarding its own prosecutors. The finding validates concerns about the NPA while stopping short of establishing accountability.

**Our editorial judgment:** The 2008 Non-Prosecution Agreement enabled Jeffrey Epstein to avoid federal accountability for sex trafficking of minors for over a decade. The documentary record supports characterizing this as one of the most consequential failures of federal prosecution in recent American history. The question of why this outcome occurred—whether due to poor judgment, resource constraints, deference to wealth and legal talent, or other factors—remains a matter of public concern.

## Alternative Interpretations

A reader reviewing the same documentary record might reasonably conclude:

1. **Prosecutorial discretion is intentionally broad.** The U.S. Attorney's Office may have made reasonable professional judgments about evidence quality, trial risks, witness availability, and resource constraints. Sex crimes cases involving minor victims present significant evidentiary challenges, and prosecutors must weigh the trauma of trial testimony against certainty of outcome. The decision to accept a state plea may have reflected legitimate concerns about the strength of the federal case.

2. **The 11th Circuit ruling is dispositive on the legal question.** The en banc reversal of Judge Marra's ruling establishes that no CVRA violation occurred as a matter of law. If victims had no statutory rights at the pre-charging stage, then prosecutors cannot have violated those rights. The Eleventh Circuit's interpretation should govern assessment of the prosecutors' conduct.

3. **The OPR finding exonerates more than it condemns.** The Office of Professional Responsibility explicitly found no professional misconduct and no legal violations. "Poor judgment" is a lesser finding that acknowledges disagreement with the decision while stopping well short of finding it improper. Many prosecutorial decisions could be characterized as reflecting "poor judgment" in hindsight.

4. **The NPA did result in incarceration and registration.** Epstein served 13 months in county jail and registered as a sex offender. While critics characterize this as lenient, it was more than zero accountability. Acosta argued that without the NPA, the case might have resulted in no conviction at all. This counterfactual cannot be definitively resolved.

5. **The 2019 prosecution reflected different evidence, not the same case.** The SDNY charges against Epstein may have been supported by evidence that was not available or as developed in 2008. The Miami Herald's investigative journalism, additional victim cooperation, and a changed legal and social environment may have strengthened the federal case in ways not possible a decade earlier.

6. **Co-conspirator immunity may have served legitimate purposes.** Blanket immunity provisions in plea agreements can encourage cooperation from individuals who might otherwise invoke Fifth Amendment rights. The decision not to publicly identify protected individuals may have reflected legitimate privacy or safety concerns rather than improper intent.

7. **The NPA was reviewed and approved through normal DOJ channels.** The agreement was not the unilateral act of a single rogue prosecutor. It was reviewed within the U.S. Attorney's Office and approved by career DOJ officials. If systemic failures occurred, they implicate the institution rather than any individual.
