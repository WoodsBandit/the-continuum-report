## Executive Summary

The Epstein Florida Case refers to a series of interconnected legal proceedings arising from Jeffrey Epstein's sexual abuse of underage girls in Palm Beach, Florida, between approximately 1999 and 2007. The central controversy involves a secret Non-Prosecution Agreement (NPA) negotiated in 2008 between the U.S. Attorney's Office for the Southern District of Florida and Epstein, which shielded him from federal prosecution and was concealed from his victims in alleged violation of the Crime Victims' Rights Act (CVRA).

The primary litigation, Jane Doe #1 and Jane Doe #2 v. United States (Case No. 08-cv-80736-KAM, S.D. Fla.), was filed on July 7, 2008, and continued for over a decade, with victims seeking enforcement of their rights under 18 U.S.C. § 3771. The case established important precedent regarding victims' rights during criminal investigations, even before charges are filed.

Former U.S. Attorney Alexander Acosta, who oversaw the Southern District of Florida during the NPA negotiations, became a central figure in subsequent scrutiny of the deal's unprecedented leniency and secrecy.

### 2007-2008: Federal Investigation and Secret NPA

The U.S. Attorney's Office for the Southern District of Florida, under then-U.S. Attorney Alexander Acosta, conducted a federal investigation into Epstein's sexual abuse of minors. Rather than file federal charges, prosecutors entered into a Non-Prosecution Agreement with Epstein.

According to the Jane Doe #1 and #2 litigation, "the U.S. Attorney's Office for the Southern District of Florida had concealed from Epstein's victims a non-prosecution agreement (NPA) that they had reached with Epstein." (ECF Doc. 1330-1, filed 01/05/24, p. 2)

### July 7, 2008: CVRA Case Filed

Attorney Bradley J. (ECF Doc. 1330-1, filed 01/05/24, p. 2)

The case was captioned Jane Doe No. 1 and Jane Doe No. 2 v. United States, No. 9:08-cv-80736-KAM (S.D. Fla.).

### 2008: State Plea Deal

Epstein ultimately pleaded guilty in Florida state court to charges significantly less serious than those for which federal prosecutors had accumulated evidence. The terms of the plea included work release, allowing him to continue operating his business interests during his incarceration.

### 2011-2013: Discovery Battles

On October 11, 2011, the victims filed discovery requests with the Government, including requests specifically seeking information about Alan Dershowitz, Prince Andrew, and others. (ECF Doc. 1330-1, filed 01/05/24, p. 2)

The Government resisted producing discovery, ultimately leading to a Court ruling in June 2013 that the Government should produce documents. The Government then produced "about 1,500 pages of largely irrelevant materials to the victims" while simultaneously submitting "14,825 pages of relevant materials under seal to the Court," claiming privilege. ### 2013: Landmark CVRA Ruling

In a detailed ruling, the Court concluded that the CVRA extended rights to Jane Doe #1 and Jane Doe #2 even though federal charges were never filed. The Court explained that because the NPA barred prosecution of crimes committed against them by Epstein, they had "standing" to assert violations of the CVRA rights. (ECF Doc. 1325-3, filed 01/04/24, p. 2)

This ruling established important precedent. As noted in legal scholarship cited in court documents: "Crime Victims' Rights During Criminal Investigations? ## The Non-Prosecution Agreement: Key Aspects

### Secrecy

According to court filings, the defining feature of the NPA was its secret nature. The Government negotiated and finalized the agreement without notifying Epstein's victims, in alleged violation of their CVRA right to confer with prosecutors and receive reasonable notice of proceedings.

### Scope of Immunity

The NPA reportedly provided Epstein with immunity from federal prosecution for the sexual abuse crimes committed in Florida. The agreement also allegedly extended immunity to potential co-conspirators.

### State Plea as Substitute

Rather than face federal charges that could have resulted in decades of imprisonment, Epstein pleaded guilty to state charges in Florida. The substantially reduced charges resulted in a sentence that included work release privileges.

## The Crime Victims' Rights Act (CVRA) Framework

The CVRA, codified at 18 U.S.C. § 3771, establishes specific rights for victims of federal crimes, including:

- The right to reasonable, accurate, and timely notice of any public court proceeding
- The right to confer with the attorney for the Government in the case
- The right to be treated with fairness and with respect for the victim's dignity and privacy

According to court documents in the Jane Doe #1 and #2 case, "the CVRA can apply to protect crime victims' rights even before an indictment is filed." (ECF Doc. 1330-1, filed 01/05/24, p. 2)

This represented a significant expansion of victims' rights jurisprudence. Prior to the ruling in the Epstein CVRA case, the Government had argued that "CVRA rights did not apply to Jane Doe #1 and Jane Doe #2 because no federal charges had ever been filed against Jeffrey Epstein." The Court firmly rejected this position. (ECF Doc. 1325-3, filed 01/04/24, p. 2)
